This such an important conversation. My personal minimum standard ask for door deals is always a 70% cut of the door to the artists from $1. I just won’t take a ticketed show that offers less because it ends up being too much work to promote. There are exceptions I make but that’s just my standard.

Here are my thoughts on the idea of asking First Ave to change the local artists door deal.

1) Do we know if everyone on the local scene is getting the same door deal? I played a local show there in January 2024 and I believe the door deal was 60/40 in favor of the artists. If they are inconsistent, perhaps asking them to come up with a standard door deal with artists would be step 1. We get into some shitty territory in terms of pay equity in the absence of a standard, so I think this is an really important piece.

2) In a low turn out scenario, a 55% of the door from $1 sounds alright to me. First Ave does a lot to help artists get a good turn out including: print posters, support promo, the sound quality is always excellent. Not to mention they have to staff security and door folks (huge shout out to the incredible folks who staff their venues- they deserve to be paid well). All this to say, They’re running a really well greased machine with a lot of overhead. That means If there’s a low turn out at a show, First Ave takes a really big hit. I’d imagine local shows can be extra risky because draw can vary so much. If I only bring 10 folks in the door they’re gonna lose money big time, ya know? And I’d still get my cut. And feel pretty grateful!

If there’s a kick ass turn out though….55% of the door no longer sounds so good! So maybe instead of advocating for a higher cut from $1 , we could advocate for a tiered system.
This could maybe look something like this:
First 100 tickets sold: 65/35 from $1 in favor of artist
100-200 tickets sold: 70/30 from $1 in favor of artist
200-300 tickets 75/25 from $1 in favor of artist
300+ tickets from $1 80/20 from $1 in favor of artist

When I was living in Omaha, I went to a book reading event by Richard Boch about The Mudd Club, which was a Brooklyn-based venue in the late 70s and early 80s. At the end of the reading, he opened it up for questions. I asked him something like, “Do you think this kind of venue could happen in Brooklyn now?” He said he didn’t think it could—there’s too much gentrification now. I didn’t record the conversation, but I think he might have said that it might be more possible in the Midwest now. (This was about 8 years ago if memory serves). The reason I bring this up is because it makes me think of the term venue gentrification. I’m probably not the first person to use this term, but everything about First Avenue makes me think of it. Organic band scenes are getting harder and harder to get going, and this is due to a whirlwind of negative factors like late stage capitalism, record companies not signing bands anymore, Spotify in general (this is a big one), gentrification, social media stealing our in person interactions, and now the rise of American Fascism, stagnant minimum wage (think capitalism), and so on and so forth.

What I personally believe First Avenue and Spotify want people to think is that bands are a thing of the past. A novelty. If a band doesn’t do well on Spotify, well fuck I guess people just don’t like bands anymore. If bands aren’t being signed or else gaining momentum in mainstream society, then who’s going to give a shit? Record companies and Spotify are in bed together. Mainstream radio? Forget about it. Record sales? Why buy the record if I can stream it? Live shows? Well they’re not famous so they must be trash. Here’s the kicker—they’re not famous BECAUSE of Spotify and BECAUSE of record labels. And why is this? I have talked about this elsewhere on this site, but in my personal opinion it boils down to the fact that bands are seen as a menace to hegemonic society—I mean, look at what all those god damn hippies did in the sixties and weren’t they all in bands?

I have no proof of this, but I do think that Spotify + Record labels = mass exclusion of bands from everything. They corner bands at every turn—the music they make, the things they sell, the performances they do. And by doing so they snuff out the flame of what really matters: the messages they send. What’s better, 10,000 bands doing what they want and making money and saying things or 6 superfamous solo acts? After all, its easier to control 6 people. Just saying.

I stayed in the Midwest partially because of what Richard Boch said about gentrification. I thought that maybe something neat could spark somewhere in the Midwest that wasn’t on the coasts and that had enough geographical space to happen. Bands and music need space. They need a supportive city, supportive people, and supportive venues. For First Ave to jump on this gentrification train, well, the train’s going the wrong way. I honestly think record companies and Spotify want people to think that organic scenes don’t exist or soon will not exist. Like I’ve said in previous posts, I don’t have any concrete answers, and I won’t pretend to. I personally feel that there has to be some way to stop measuring success on platforms created by what turns out to be fascists and instead find strength from each other. Sorry about the length of this.

I don’t think I was making excuses for poor compensation. Just sharing my perspective based off of my conversations with venues regarding why/how they justify keeping such a large cut. I personally will rarely take a door deal that’s less than 70% to artists and I’m a huge proponent of artists never taking less than this on a door deal. That said, I can understand and appreciate that a large venue like First Ave might need to keep a larger cut if they are unsure of an artists/events ability to sell tickets. It takes a lot of staff to open for a show and they have to make sure they’re able to cover their expenses. It’s just business. That’s why I think a tiered system strikes a good compromise. It keeps the risk lower for the venue while still advocating for higher pay for the artists. First Ave is never gonna want to go in the hole on a show, so whatever is proposed is going to have to make sense from a business perspective… otherwise they will just say “nope. Next!”
