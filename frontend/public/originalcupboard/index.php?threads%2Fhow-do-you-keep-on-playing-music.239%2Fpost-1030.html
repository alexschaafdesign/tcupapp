i'm sorry that you're feeling discouraged. burnout is so real and it's so hard to get back on track once it sets in. i have a full time job outside of music, so that's helped on the finances side of the equation, but it also often leaves me feeling like i'm behind everyone else.

in order to stay focused on making art at my own pace, i try to remember that music gives me joy, even when it sounds like garbage. other stuff, like the value of music and art, etc. all that stuff i have to keep at a distance, keep it at a philosophical level, or i lose myself in how awful everything is.

don't know if it helps, but here's my thinking after a long, long time getting mad about all the dumb stuff that i can't control: "value" is an insidious word. capitalism only values something insofar as it can make money. the state only values something insofar as it reinforces rules and institutions of the state. yet somehow "value" also denotes a system of individual ethical and moral principles.

i think it can be a trap to think about things that make us feel (art) in terms of "value" precisely because the structural stuff weasels its way into to our thought processes. "value" makes us conceptualize our labor--the work of making art, expending energy toward achieving artistic goals, even the willingness to call a project "complete"--through the lens of external, structural forces, especially profitability.

"value" as it exists today often makes it excruciatingly difficult to pursue labor that gives us joy. the internalization of "value systems" directs our experiences under these structural forces into channels where the only answer is to produce more "value" for them (e.g. art is a hobby and artists should have "real" jobs). coupled with an unrelenting music industry that moves at the speed of likes, clicks, and follower counts, we're left with this unnerving feeling that we're always three steps behind where we should be.

i don't know if there's any way to reconcile this reality with any immediacy, but it's part of the reason why i'm so excited about tcup's potential and feel drawn to the underground like @thunderik.

p.s. good mourning, i love you is amazing on so many fronts. love, love, love it.

I was (mostly) a full-time touring musician between 2013-2021. Shutdown + burnout hit pretty hard, and while I kept doing residency + seasonal shows I switched careers entirely and stopped regular touring. To echo some of the above, having diversified income offers a lot more financial stability.

For me, it's still very difficult to accept. It helps a bit to remember that, for many, a career in music isn't just writing/playing/recording/etc. It's booking and promotion and logistics and sales and a whole lot of other stuff that (for me) isn't really at all about art, and I still wouldn't like even if it were more financially stable.

The "other stuff" is especially tricky in MSP, I think, because we notably lack established "talent management" infrastructure. So many bookers/promoters/etc are musicians doing it temporarily as a side-gig. Nothing wrong with that, but very few career professionals.

Recently, the process of switching over to writing and performing without a focus on money (for probably the first time in almost 20 years) has been really great. I've been enjoying writing and playing in a way I haven't since I was a teenager.

Still, it doesn't feel right. Especially after you've put the majority of your life into your craft and art, there should be in infrastructure to support that. We SHOULD just be able to do what we love and work hard at.

Hopefully TCUP is a way for us to make that a more accessible reality in the future.

I feel this. It's not very sustainable, emotionally and financially, to operate in that grind~burnout way... it's so dumb the way the industry cogs still demand.it after decades of it not working for anyone other than ceos of huge record labels and streaming services.
A lot of times I have to remind myself to radically and directly refuse any feelings of guilt around unreasonable and predatory industry expectations.

Leaning into organizing with TCUP has been a huge source of re-energizing as a musician. It has allowed me to feel connected to our local art and music scene without feeling like I have to grind and play a bunch of shows just to get/stay connected or be 'relevant'.

Straight up band practice--not practicing for a show but just for us, creating together, helps a ton.

Having something like a musicians Basic Income program, seeing what $ resources we can tap into through MPLS arts and cultural affairs, creating our own mutual aid fund, having closer access to grants... Having structure for folks who are full time musicians to take a break... There are lots of areas performers need more support to be emotionally & physically well, validated and seen.

I definitely hear you -- it sucks that, for now (as others have said above), "try to have most of your income come from non-music jobs" is maybe the closest thing to a 'right answer' that i've found, as far as how to improve one's mental health as a musician in the immediate present -- a temporary band-aid until we figure out how to make things better, financially.

It's obviously not right that "being a full-time musician" gives no promise of financial security; but for me at least I've found that if i can manage to not depend on music for the main part of my income (which is not easy, i know its not a simple fix), i gain a lot more headspace + energy to be able to think about music in a neutral/positive way, and also be able to dedicate time to organizing/tcup etc, which feels fulfiilling in its own way --obviously it means i have less time to devote to music in a given day, but i think "i'm pretty energized, and i have 90 minutes to work on songs" works better for me than "i have all day, but i'm fucking exhausted and stressed". (though obviously a day job can ALSO make you exhausted and stressed)

the most desolate i've felt is at the end of like a 9 week tour playing in someone else's band, when music felt like such a drag and like i'm contributing to a very flawed ecosystem - so i totally understand the feeling. no easy answer, but keep going, we need you!

Hilary!

I've been attempting to reframe everything I make as "For Archival Purposes" so my processes/work doesn't cease completely.
So its: make now, finish whenever your standards smile, put it in the bank and release 6mo/year/decade later...or when it feels good again to do so.
Anything I make has my heart and soul in it, but I'm not making it for anything in particular.

I've also been stocking up on small moments as well: little duet videos with friends, solo vids of favorite tunes, and making vibe-y little video montages with my own music overlayed. I release some of it, but a lot I put in a Dropbox folder for later.

During covid/lockdown times I found such great catharsis going back to my pages of notes & hundreds of little demos I made in the years prior. I forgot so many of them, but suddenly I was surrounded by things that were familiar and also uniquely my own. (Although re-learning songs you forgot is a weird challenge sometimes) I'm hoping this current "Archival Period' will help me out down the line.

Right now I'm publicizing new music every few months with very little fanfare or shows to promote. I feel like it's a little ecosystem/lizard terrarium I've built and if people like my little bugs&guys runnin' around they can tap on the glass.

I feel you so much Hillary. Like you I gave my all for 10+ years to try to make creating my own music a self sustaining career. After coming to the point where I realized that it wasn't going to happen in the way I wanted it to, I definitely went through a lengthy period of "what's the point." The thing is that musical ideas, and the desire to express them just never fully went away, and eventually I just started following those impulses again. Now I finally think I've come to the point where I see that holding back that impulse just amplifies that feeling of pointlessness. That isn't to say that I don't still periodically want to sell all of my gear and just give it up all together, but each time I at least can remember that I feel most myself when I'm working on a tune.

The hardest part for me has become playing shows. I still love playing live, but I have a good amount of anxiety when it comes to promoting shows. To be honest, TCUP has really been inspiring me to get back to it. It just feels great to be part of a supportive community of musicians again that are sharing their struggles and sharing encouragement. I will say that I'm such a huge fan of you're music, and have been since we played our first show together (wasn't that in Northfield with Michael Morris). I can't wait to hear more of the wonderful ideas buzzing around your brain.
