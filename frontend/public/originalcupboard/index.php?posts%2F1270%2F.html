[existential rambling] -- is "not enough people want to come out to shows" sort of an under-discussed Big Issue (along with fair pay)?

this isn’t necessarily a fully fleshed out thought, but something i’ve been puzzling over for a while — along with getting paid fairly as workers, is “not enough people want to go to shows” = true? and if so, is it kind of an equally big puzzle to figure out? curious if anyone else has a reaction to this, or a counterpoint…

i obvs don’t mean ‘live music in general is in trouble’, clearly the top 1% are doing fine, packing out stadiums, etc. but those of us in this ‘middle class’ or whatever, it feels harder and harder to get turnout most of the time, which can sometimes make "getting paid fairly" feel beside the point a little bit...

like, i can get a good door deal at a venue (80/20 or whatever), and if the room was packed i would have been paid quite nicely, but if only 20 people come, i’m not paid well (and can’t in good conscience say ‘i should have been paid the same as if it was sold out’?) — and i also probably didn't have as much FUN as i would with a packed room, money aside. So even if i was guaranteed $500 for that show by the city/state govt etc, that would be SICK and a huge improvement, but not necessarily fixing all of the issues and not making things as fun/fulfilling as they could be.

(obviously this is not to discount at ALL our current tcup campaign, it’s a smart first move to start with things more in our control (get venues to treat us fairly, pay us well) and to demand things that we definitely deserve)

but like, i think we all know the struggle of ‘we have a show, let’s try to get all of our friends to come’ rather than something like ‘we booked a show at this super popular spot which is usually packed full of music fans, regardless of the bands, hell yeah’ which is sort of my musical utopia vision.

so many shows where it’s like “the only people in the crowd are the other bands’ which is still fun and i like playing music, but i’ve also had a lot of ‘Wait….who am i doing this FOR?’

and yes i can often blame the venues for not promoting the show well, but it doesn’t usually feel like 'people are out there desperate to see us play, and as long as they KNOW about the show they will come’ so more promotion wouldn’t necessarily be a perfect fix.

i do think its a fixable problem though — just because its hard to get turnout for a show with our current system doesn’t mean it’d be equally hard after changing the way things work.

i dont know what an answer would look like, but maybe stuff like
  • less traditional “shows that go from 8-11pm at a venue that serves alcohol”
  • more house shows
  • more daytime shows??
  • slightly more out there / half-formed and i'll probably disagree with this later, but -- what if there were slightly less venues (get rid of the shittier ones) so that it was slightlyharder to book a show in town, and we maybe play a few less shows per year, but the ones that we DO play are better attended, better paid, and more fun? ehhh i don't know!
    • Don't mean this in a gatekeep-y way at all, there should always be opportunities for people to play in front of people wherever and whenever they want, just spitballing
  • more/better press, radio etc focused on the local music scene
thank you for reading my novel, rant over

I am just going to toss my opinion in the mix-- and keep in mind this is just an opinion. 1991 was over 30 years ago. Nirvana got rid of all the trashy bloated crap going on. Punk came out on top-- at least for the time being. Shit was thriving. The more successful bands are in culture, the harder time the establishment has with dealing-- which is a good thing. What the establishment has figured out is that bands and the culture surrounding them create real problems for them, so they're always looking for ways to diminish band culture, punk, whatever you want to call it. Enter Spotify--most recently anyway. "Bands aren't getting plays on Spotify anymore so they must not be good/relevant so their shows must not be that great, I mean just look at how few streams they have compared to my favorite pop star" etc. This is all to say-- when a group of bands or a band starts getting traction in mainstream culture, it paves a way for other bands or underground artists to get through that busted down door. And the way streaming is now, it's becoming harder and harder for bands to break the door down. I dunno-- maybe a lot of this is pretty old hat for most of the folks on here. But I think the moment "mainstream culture" starts realizing that underground, independent artists are actually saying and doing shit that matters and should be paid attention to, people might start flocking to shows. Do I really know? Nope. Do I have a solution or answer? No. I just know that the minute people stop seeing bands as "cute" and see them as necessary instead, things could change for the better.

There's something of a double-edged sword thing going on here. On the one hand, we're in a late-capitalist media landscape that is extremely hostile to the entire concept of live music. The guitar band is dead, the monoculture is dead, short-form video content is the medium of the day, people are more alienated and antisocial, we're at the tail end of a long Hollowing of the Middle where everything has flowed to the top 1% and it's harder than ever to claw out a niche living with your indie project, etc etc. DIY/indie live music is a subculture. And of course the pandemic put a huge wrench into everything.

But on the other hand, we are still one of the strongest independent music scenes in the entire country and there are absolutely still people who go to shows and see live music. There are like five shows on any given weeknights, weekends you could have nearly 40 shows sometimes. Other scenes around the country aren't all like that. If anything, the average showgoer has so many options that they might be sacrificing one for another. New people go to their first show every day! So from all this I gather:
  • Make a personal connection with your fans and friends at the show and make them want to come out!
  • Choose/assemble strong bills that are a good fit for the venue
  • Understand every show's a bit different and that's cool and all part of the game

Probably the worst question a booker can ask when a band or artist is inquiring about a gig is, "How many people can you bring in?" The standard answer from the musicians should always be, "How the fuck do I know." There are so many factors involved as to why people do or do not go see live music. The weather, especially in the winter, the start time, how far away is the venue, are they really motivated or are they just a casual music fan and so on. I started playing around the Twin Cities in the late 1980's and there were a lot less entertainment options plus it was less common for people to work two jobs thus being too tired to leave the house or at that other job. I've been in bands where as an opener we have brought in more people than the headliners and the next time around 10 people showed up. The venues shouldn't be preoccupied with how many people we bring in because that number should be icing on the cake. I've always wondered what the owners does during the other nights there isn't live music to bring in customers. Do they just take what they can get. The thing is, we come in to play, we bring in people who might not normally frequent the place and then we get penalized IMO when they take a percentage of more than 10% of the door and even a flat fee on top of that. How about you just accept the extra money you make from selling the drinks and food that night that the people who normally wouldn't be there just bought and not take up to 50% of the band's revenue.

Anyway, I went off subject. How do we get more people to show up? I think that's a question for which no one realistically has a good answer. I have talked with enough of my fellow musicians over the decades where they all experienced audience number fluctuations. Maybe they were playing too often or not often enough for example. Were they still in the friends and family zone and not growing their base to include strangers? Eventually that's what we all have to do but it can be easier said than done. I'm still after all these years working on that. Every time you start a new band it's back to square one. Right now, the band I'm currently in is looking at organizing our own gigs outside of the bar network. We played a private party a couple of weeks ago with a few other bands and about 100 people attended. We handed out our cards and we're looking into doing another multi band event. We have been contacting venues for April (most places are already booked up for February and March) and still haven't gotten a reply. Saturation is a big issue. Music equipment sales shot up during Covid and lots of bands formed once people could be in the same room. Our current drummer bought a kit during this time and taught himself to play. Pre Covid when I would book a band, I usually heard back within 72 hours from the venue. Now it can be weeks if you even hear back at all. It makes me wonder if they aren't picking and choosing based on social media activity and so on instead of first contact first serve. I get that because of the financial reason but artist also need to get a start somewhere. The venues need to take more chances sometimes. My advice is maybe hold some gigs on your own. Can you have parties where you or bandmates live and tap all the members of the band to invite everyone they know? It sounds simplistic but it could work. Try organizing something in an unconventional place. That then gives you leverage when you book a bar gig. If you get to the point where you know for sure you can bring in 50 or 100 let the venue know.
