Is the Twin Cities Music Scene Back to Normal After COVID?

A post-pandemic quest to find the unified local music scene.
mspmag.com

the article is behind a paywall but i hacked the system:
pt1:
This is never a feel-good exercise—in fact, it’s more like a guaranteed feel-bad exercise—but humor me for a second and think back to the pandemic. Like, close your eyes, and really think back to the claustrophobic, people-are-strange-when-you’re-a-stranger, peak-socially-distanced lockdown: There you are, on your couch, maybe emerging from another soul-crushing Zoom, maybe about to cue up a soul-crushing episode of Tiger King or mind-numbing binge of Ted Lasso. Did you ever despair that things would never get back to normal? Can you remember daydreaming about what “back to normal” would look like? And was your vision anything like mine—a packed Mainroom show at First Avenue?

Four years later, I can report, from a packed show at First Avenue, we’re back. Well, maybe not sardine-can, 1,550-capacity back, but pretty back, especially for a show that started after 9 p.m. on a Sunday. Here I am, stage left under the stairs, shoulder-to-shoulder with my 45-to-54 demo, collectively revved up on gummies and IPAs, sweating through our flannels. The Oscar-nominated actor Michael Shannon is on stage, wearing all black—T-shirt and jeans, watch cap—fronting a tight, very guitar-y band led by Chicago musician Jason Narducy and hipster drummer extraordinaire Jon Wurster. Shannon is going full method on R.E.M. frontman Michael Stipe, skittering in place, his two hands cradling his microphone, mimicking Stipe’s stilted croon on “Talk About the Passion”—“Not! Everyone! Can carry the weight of the world...”

Four years ago, it was fair to wonder if we would ever get back to this. And yes, seeing Shannon’s mumbled lyric-for-mumbled lyric facsimile of R.E.M.’s 1983 debut Murmur is contributing to the “living-in-a-simulation” vibes (history note: The last time the actual R.E.M. alt-rocked the Mainroom was pre-Murmur, in 1982). Yet, while we are all a little grayer, and slightly paunchier than I imagined, this is the scene I hoped for, way back on my COVID couch.

But are we really back? Is packing the room for an incredibly committed R.E.M. cover band evidence of a comeback, or something more decrepit? With all the cultural headwinds facing musicians and the music industry—the rise of streaming, the end of the album review, the rise of Ticketmaster’s “dynamic pricing,” and the $1,000 stadium show ticket—is a room full of veteran rock fans enjoying 40-year-old songs evidence that we’re back, or that we’re still missing something, or that we’ve evolved into something new?

pt 2:
It’s been four years since First Avenue’s owner and CEO Dayna Frank told me, in a profile that ran in this magazine, “We’re one of the only independent promoters in the country with the balls to take on Live Nation.” She gave me that quote two months before the multinational, artist-managing, venue-operating, concert-promoting behemoth was about to open its own 1,962-cap club, The Fillmore, less than a mile away from First Avenue’s Mainroom. The Fillmore (Live Nation’s ninth Fillmore in the U.S.) did open that winter, for about a month, before COVID shut it down. But now, it’s regularly sold out, a jewel in a Live Nation crown that encircles everything from shows at the Varsity Theater (Live Nation–run since 2018) to shows at the new Uptown Theater (it works on bookings with Uptown’s owner and developer Ned Abdul) to its big, nationwide tours that bring stars like Madonna to arenas like the X. The cage match between First Avenue and Live Nation encompasses our most important music venues and the health of our local performance scene.

Over a similar time period, First Avenue’s Frank has become a national force as well, primarily by leading her industry’s lobby for the $15 billion Save Our Stages Act, the bipartisan legislation authored by Minnesota’s Democratic Senator Amy Klobuchar and her Republican counterpart from Texas, Senator John Cornyn. Indie venue owners across the country credit Save Our Stages with not only saving their industry during the pandemic (First Avenue itself received $8 million from the bill) but improving their chances to stay open in the face of Live Nation’s monopoly money. Frank is now board president of the National Independent Venue Association, or NIVA, which originally formed in April 2020 to lobby for the legislation. NIVA has become a permanent trade organization with a local chapter, MNIVA, that includes 70 venues.

First Avenue is the second-largest conglomerate among MNIVA members (Hennepin Theatre Trust is the largest, with its three historic theaters and Dudley Riggs Theatre). And First Avenue is, in fact, successfully competing with Live Nation. It starts with the deep, sentimental attachment that many touring artists have to the room that Purple Rain built (check out ecstatic social media posts by visiting artists like Jon Batiste or Jason Isbell). Frank and her seasoned management team have leveraged that sentiment, and by extending their control to six total venues, they now offer booking agents the stages to accommodate almost any sized tour. According to First Avenue’s marketing VP Ashley Ryan, First Avenue booked 1,200 shows across its six venues in 2023. “Which is back to the volume of 2018 and 2019,” she says.

Live Nation declined to grant me an interview, but market president Josh Lacey did text me a statement. “I’ve been here for more than a decade, and I’m particularly proud that we’re contributing to a growing live music scene in the Twin Cities,” it read, in part. “The demand for live experiences is stronger than ever, and you see that here locally with increased ticket sales.” There is solid evidence supporting both First Avenue’s and Live Nation’s claims that the Twin Cities concert market is recovering from COVID. Concert industry publication Pollstar has the Twin Cities ranked as the 17th-largest concert market in the country, with $129 million in gross ticket sales in 2022, up from just under $40 million the year before, which means we’re roughly on a trajectory to where we were in 2019.

And yes, a lot of those dollars are going to big tickets for stadium and arena tour stops by the likes of Taylor Swift and Bruce Springsteen, but this is where the Twin Cities market behaves a little differently: In 2022, we sold almost half a million more tickets than markets with similar gross sales numbers. Meaning the enthusiastic music lovers of the Twin Cities are more frequently going to smaller shows. Ryan says First Avenue’s comeback in particular remains fueled by the 25-to-34-year-old demo. “Those are the folks that are going out the most,” she says. Older folks go out less, she says, but when they do, they’re spending money on things like VIP experiences that include cushier seats, artist access, or merchandise packages. And, she notes, it’s taken until 2023 to see older demos, like my own, return to pre-pandemic levels.

Like a Gen X Rip Van Winkle, I wondered what else changed while we were asleep? My generation aged out of the “It” ticket buying crowd. Duh. But did the younger people attending lots of shows still care about local music—or maybe the bigger question should be, should young people still care about local music? “I don’t think we can have a healthy local music economy without local artists being supported by local venues,” Ryan says. First Avenue continues to consider our local music ecosystem—people here are socialized to love music by going to small shows in small venues, usually played by people they know—to be what their mini empire was founded on. Ryan touts this institutional value by arguing it now has the stages to accommodate the growth of a local artist from their first big local show (7th Street Entry, Turf Club) to their first silver-star-making turn (First Avenue’s Mainroom) to their ascension to theater-filling force (Palace Theatre). And if an artist gets bigger than that, it can even book them into an “open room,” like the 6,500-cap Armory (which local band Hippo Campus sold out in spring 2022), or, within a couple of years, at its own 8,000-plus-seat Upper Harbor Amphitheater.

But as First Avenue, the company, not the venue, has expanded and ramped up to compete with multinationals like Live Nation, it is booking far more national and international touring acts. If you look at its online calendar, you’ll still see a ton of local shows (each one with two little stars denoting it as such) but also a deluge of touring acts. Ryan concedes that, although First Avenue hosts more local shows than any similar-sized production company in the country, those shows are now spread across six stages. Find one of those vintage First Avenue print concert calendars—they occasionally pop up on my social media—and you’ll see that, in previous decades, local bands populated the majority of its original two stages, First Avenue and 7th Street Entry. On any given night, local musicians and their fans can still be found at one or more of First Avenue’s clubs, but it’s not as routine to encounter the unified local music scene there on a night in, night out basis. And maybe “the unified scene” was only ever an intangible ideal (from a fantastic Hold Steady lyric) in the first place, but in this era of constant ambient FOMO, finding everybody hanging out together feels more elusive than it ever has.

pt 3:

My buddy Mark Mallman is a musician who’s been making a living off music for more than two decades since emigrating from Milwaukee to graduate from the Minneapolis College of Art and Design. “If you remove the idea of an average income from the equation and you live cheap,” he jokes, “you can be a professional musician for as long as you want.” He generates his income on a variety of fronts: as a commercial composer, and as a rock ’n’ roll streamer and performer. His new thing is TikTok—almost 100,000 users follow his brilliant two-minute pop song exegeses.

“From banging on a rock to using a distortion pedal, music has always been a technology-driven medium,” Mallman explains. “And right now, streaming and social media technology are dictating the music people make.” He says software like GarageBand and Ableton makes it cheaper and easier to record music than ever before, rendering traditional barriers to its production and distribution—record labels, radio stations, etc.—as either irrelevant or less powerful. The result? More than 1,000,000 songs being uploaded to Spotify every month. “But if 1,000,000 songs are being uploaded a month, your supply of artists who want to play shows is bigger than ever,” he says. “It’s getting back to being a folk art again.”

Mallman thinks this is why so many small—90-to-150-cap—music venues have opened recently. This year, we’ve seen Berlin in North Loop, Zhora Darling in Northeast, Cloudland on Lake Street, and the White Rock Lounge in Lowertown St. Paul. Before that, there was the White Squirrel in St. Paul, and the Green Room and the Granada Theater in Uptown. You can find a specific niche at each place—jazz at Berlin, hard core at Cloudland, twangy Americana at White Squirrel—while each place is open to other micro scenes. Ryan says these rooms are filling a market gap that probably existed even before COVID killed the Kitty Kat Club in Dinkytown, or the Hexagon in Seward. And it warms my heart to read a recent Star Tribune article on the house-show scene—cramped, student-run brment shows off Como Avenue, the primordial ooze from which every first band emerges. Showing up at a brment show with a backwards brball cap asking, “How do you do, fellow kids?” like in that Steve Buscemi meme might’ve been entertaining, but it’s probably more productive just to trace my steps back to the last place I felt like I regularly participated in the local music scene: Icehouse.

Twelve years ago, Brian Liebeck and Matthew Bickford opened up a 250-cap venue/gastropub. Liebeck was an IT infrastructure guy at Best Buy with years of experience in stage production, and Bickford was the owner of Be’Wiched, a once-beloved deli in the North Loop. They were looking to open a 100-cap venue when Peter Remes proposed Bickford open up a Be’Wiched as an anchor tenant for his Eat Street development.

“I was like, ‘No way we’re doing Nicollet,’” remembers Liebeck in between sets when I stop into Icehouse. Liebeck had invited me to check out a show by tap dancer Kaleena Miller, backed by drummer (and new Icehouse booker) JT Bates, singer Aby Wolf, bass player Cody McKinney, and piano player Bryan Nichols. Liebeck seemed stoked that 150 tap dance/jazz aficionados showed up for a seated show on a Wednesday night. It jogs a memory back to that first visit to the space, when he thought it way too big and out of their budget. “Fuck it,” he remembers saying. “Let’s try it.”

The jump from 100 cap to 250 cap/full restaurant was a daunting one. Liebeck remembers there being a lull in the music scene back then. Favorite haunts like the Clown Lounge and the Dinkytowner had closed, and legendary Rhymesayers rapper Eyedea had overdosed, casting a pall on the hip-hop scene. So, when Icehouse opened, a bunch of scenes simultaneously adopted it: jazzers, rappers, DJs, all mixed up with hipsters who liked noise as much as funk. Liebeck’s connections to Clown Lounge’s experimental music scene meant he had a rotating group of players that would play during dinner, and when service ended, there was a rush to clear the floor of tables for late-night shows starting at 11 p.m. (I can’t believe I attended so many 11 p.m. shows.)

Liebeck says he now realizes that the two halves of his business were fighting each other—the restaurant was overshadowed by the venue, and the restaurant pushed those set times back too far. When the refurbished Parkway started eating into its draw by putting on 7 p.m. shows, Liebeck says the strain was noticeable.

And then, in March 2020, after almost a decade in business, COVID closed Icehouse for months. The team scrambled to reopen on Memorial Day weekend. “The weekend of the Floyd murder,” he remembers. Nicollet was engulfed in chaos. As that nightmare summer dragged on, Liebeck thought about closing for good. Instead, after buying time with an infusion of Paycheck Protection Program money, he decided to lean into being a venue. “That was the magic of the place,” he says. “If we had been another restaurant closing, it would’ve been another restaurant closing—but closing a music venue would’ve stung.”

He parted ways with Bickford, and gained a low-key husband-and-wife team of investors who are passionate about the arts. Now they’ve sunk $600,000 into dramatically upgrading the sound system and remodeling the venue’s layout, expanding its capacity to 400. With his old Clown Lounge running partner Bates now booking the room, Liebeck hopes to bring in more touring acts. His candid opinion is the local music scene is amidst another lull, but he’s clearly not betting against it—more like hedging. Liebeck admits to a myriad of missteps over the years—he’s dealt with post-COVID staff in-fighting, and says he regrets having to cancel a Latinx queer dance night to book a Chappelle after-party—but despite taking the heat, he hasn’t lost the fire to do this. Although he welcomes the saner 8 p.m. start times. “Sometimes I think I’m crazy for going all in,” he says. “But I love it.”

And then, as this story went to press, the Star Tribune reported that Icehouse’s Chicago-brd landlord was suing them for $85,000 in unpaid rent. All of a sudden, the rosy renovation vibes had soured—would Icehouse even be able to stay open into the summer? In July, Liebeck invited me out to the club to explain what happened with the lawsuit and to tell me he and his new partners had just signed a new 28-month lease.

“The lawsuit hit a week after your article came out,” Liebeck says. “So we were all wondering if [the landlord] saw it and said, fuck this!” Liebeck says the lease was always going to get renegotiated—both sides knew beginning on January 1, 2024, the rent was increasing by $8,000 a month, and Icehouse’s position was that in the post-COVID market, this high of an increase would be unworkable.

“In December the cost of everything was going up—food, booze, labor—and we had lost some money on some big shows that fall. So I called them and said, ‘Hey guys, I’m struggling and we need to talk.’”

He says the building was on the market at that point, and the landlord immediately proposed kicking the can down the road and renegotiating in June, which is ultimately what ended up happening. Liebeck still doesn’t understand why the landlord sued him—“we went through COVID together”—knowing that the renegotiation could end up in the press. “We had agreed to send them our financials by April,” he says, “and literally the week our taxes were being done, the Star Tribune article comes out—I’m like, what the fuck?!”

pt 4:

With all of these local shows spread across venues new and old—each of them serving their respective niches—the music scene can feel as factionalized as any other formerly central pillar of cultural life. No doubt one of the factors contributing to this sense of decentralization is an absence of an alt-weekly devoted to music—RIP City Pages and the more recently departed Dispatch. Local curation has been supplanted by algorithms that tailor individual recommendations to subscribers. And yet, the media entity with the largest impact on the local scene continues to broadcast—in fact, The Current is nearing its 20-year anniversary.

I meet The Current’s managing director, David Safar, at my local coffee shop—PILLLAR Forum on Central Avenue (PILLLAR is a skateboarding brand and coffee shop, and moonlights as a 100-cap music venue). Safar got his start in college radio, on U of M’s Radio K, and he’s always been an advocate for the local scene as a crucial component of a healthy local music economy. He points out that, during his decade-and-a-half at the station, he’s seen The Local Show expand from one hour to five, adding Diane Miller, a local musician and journalist, to serve as host. Not that he’s without concerns. “When The Current launched, social media like Bandcamp and SoundCloud were emerging, and people were putting their events on Facebook,” he says. Nineteen years later, he doesn’t see decreased demand for music—in fact, he sees the opposite—but he sees big changes in the ways people find it. This creates profound challenges for artists who want to be discovered. “If you decide to write a song tomorrow, and you want to reach people,” he says, “it’s more mysterious than ever navigating the digital spaces.”

Safar says the big question is one everybody who has an emotional connection to music should be asking: “How are we going to support young artists and young music fans, coming up through this community, who need to find an outlet and connect with music fans beyond their own circle?” It might be easier than ever to make music, and a new stage to accommodate local musicians seems to pop up with each passing month, but with the algorithmic streamer as the dominant industry model, it’s increasingly difficult for a local musician to develop a sustainable career. Is local music being relegated to a hobby bad for the art?

After all, “We’re not talking about what’s considered the fine arts,” Safar says. Those arts seem to receive both more philanthropic, and state and city government support. “But we’re talking about music that has grown out of our community organically.” He doesn’t believe the answer is more venues or more competition between promoters or even another media outlet. “I think it’s going to be all of us working together in collaboration and partnership, and coming to the table and figuring out what are the needs and how do we help address them? How do we amplify those voices and make sure they’re heard, and then figure out a way to support them?”


Photo by Allison Garrigan
Ber at First Avenue’s Mainroom at The Current’s Winter Warmup on January 26, 2024
Ah, the artists. One of the basic planks of the venue business is the artist gets the majority of the “door”—the money paid in tickets or cover charges, and that can break down anywhere from 80/20 to a 50/50 split with the venue—and the venue makes its profits on alcohol sales. But according to First Avenue’s Ryan, this economic model is stressed by the Gen Zs, who have kinda quiet-quit drinking. “Think about it,” she says. “What were the last few years of their lives like? They didn’t go out to bars, they didn’t have parties in their dorms, they weren’t going to house shows, and they were having an insanely different college experience.” The kids were never socialized into alcohol. “And they’re conscious of the world they live in—they’re concerned about their mental health and well-being,” wary of the consequences of engaging in antisocial behaviors that alcohol promotes. Ryan says one of the solutions is to offer non-alcoholic bevs, THC bevs, and possibly more dance parties—a pedagogy brd in rave culture. But this stress provokes another question: If the pandemic disrupted the socialization of younger crowds, what did it do to younger artists?

I talked to two 25-year-old artists on the cusp, who are emerging from incredibly disparate circumstances and with dramatically different styles. Both are about to release new EPs and sound eager to perform their new songs for their growing audiences. They both have management, and they’ve both chosen to remain in Minneapolis, either despite or because of the absence of any music industry infrastructure here—there aren’t any record labels or big-time agencies. Many talented young people come to the conclusion that they have to move away, to chase the infrastructure on the coasts. Musicians like Ber and Papa Mbye have both come to appreciate the distance, the lack of any direct influence it affords them, resulting in the ability to be as weird as they want to be.

Papa Mbye hails from a Sene-Gambian family on the north side of Minneapolis. He was an illustrator and painter before friends coaxed him into freestyling over a beat. Those tight musical friendships are still what sustains his intensely frenetic musical approach, with songs that whip between grief-struck lament to dizzying self-aggrandizement, sometimes within a bar of each other. “I’m trying to be cognizant about what people react to, but that’s not what’s pushing me,” he says. “What’s pushing me is what me and my friends are reacting to, and trusting that that’ll elicit some emotional response from others as well.” He says he’s wary of being “the token Black band in Minnesota,” and is attracted to the idea of subverting his audience’s expectations. “I don’t want to make music the audience is interested in right away,” he says. “My favorite songs have pissed me off at first, before realizing I actually like this—I want to take an audience on the same journey.”


Photo by Sawyer Brice
Papa Mbye plays 7th Street Entry
Berit Dybing, stage name Ber, grew up in Bemidji before finding herself as a songwriter, first at a folk school in Trondheim, Norway (she has family nearby), and then at a prestigious music school in Leeds, England. She graduated during the pandemic in 2020, returned to the U.S., and, instead of returning to Bemidji, she isolated in her uncle’s Minneapolis brment. “It was a hard time,” she says. “Music degree, but broke as hell, no job.” Her manager urged her to participate in regular Zoom sessions with artists all over the world, and she developed her own confessional, pop-folk style. She says the big shifts in her life—parents’ divorce, getting ghosted by her English boyfriend—gave her material. And then when one of her songs, “Meant to Be,” became a viral TikTok sensation—with 15,000,000 unique impressions—Ber had an audience literally overnight. “It really meant everything to someone who wasn’t even signed yet,” she says.

Despite their different pathways, both of them sold out the Entry on their first chance. Ber’s naturally open TikTok persona remains a huge asset, and she’s steadily built up her in-person draw, from an opening slot for Norwegian singer-songwriter Ingrid to touring around the country with her own band. She ended up selling out her next local show at the 650-cap Fine Line, a big step up from the Entry.

Papa sold out his 2022 Entry debut as well, but his is a more esoteric social media presence, he’s yet to tour extensively, and he’s only performed in a few shows at home. He was set for a couple big showcases last summer, but a string of painful personal losses disrupted his momentum, and now he’s working to find it again through the creation of new work.

This is the thing: Both Ber and Papa agree that the music they’re making now needs to be shared live. Obviously, there are enough venues in this town—maybe more than ever—to accommodate this desire to perform. But you realize while talking to them that even if there weren’t, they still would be compelled to express themselves—no matter the rewards, whether monetarily or in terms of clout. It’s a reminder that, for most musicians, music isn’t just a business.

Originally published in the April 2024 issue.
